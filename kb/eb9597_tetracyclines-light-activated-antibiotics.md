## Key Points

- Tetracyclines (especially demeclocycline and doxycycline) can act as light-activated antibiotics, binding to bacterial cells and killing them upon illumination.
- Antimicrobial photodynamic inactivation (aPDI) uses nontoxic photosensitizers combined with light to destroy microbial cells via reactive oxygen species (ROS).
- Tetracyclines that do not directly photoactivate can still prevent bacterial regrowth after illumination has ceased.
- Potassium iodide can potentiate aPDI with tetracyclines; azide quenched iodine formation but not hydrogen peroxide.
- Demeclocycline (but not doxycycline) iodinated tyrosine after light activation in the presence of potassium iodide.
- Bacteria can be killed by photoactivation of tetracyclines even in the absence of oxygen, suggesting non-singlet-oxygen mechanisms.
- Blue light activation of topically applied tetracyclines may increase bactericidal effect, given that topical tetracyclines are already used clinically.
- Phototoxicity of tetracyclines, typically considered a side effect, may be harnessed therapeutically in antimicrobial photodynamic contexts.
