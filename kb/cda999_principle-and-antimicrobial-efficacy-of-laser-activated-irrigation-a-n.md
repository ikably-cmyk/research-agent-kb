## Key Points

- Laser-activated irrigation (LAI) uses pulsed erbium lasers (Er:YAG, Er;Cr:YSGG) to generate cavitation bubbles in root canal irrigants, producing intense fluid dynamics throughout the canal system.
- Cavitation is the primary mechanism of LAI: rapidly growing and imploding vapour bubbles cause high-speed fluid motion, secondary cavitation, and (in free reservoirs) shock waves.
- Key parameters influencing LAI efficacy include laser wavelength, pulse energy, pulse length, fibre tip geometry (conical vs. flat), and fibre tip position.
- Erbium lasers (Er:YAG at 2940 nm, Er;Cr:YSGG at 2780 nm) are uniquely suited for LAI due to their extremely high water absorption coefficients (13,000 and 7,000 cm⁻¹ respectively); diode lasers are not suited for cavitation-based LAI.
- Shorter pulse durations and higher pulse energies produce larger cavitation bubbles and greater mechanical energy; conical fibre tips produce spherical bubbles with higher energy density than flat tips.
- Historical trend in LAI: from intracanal fibre placement with flat tips and high pulse energies (60–80 mJ), to coronal/pulp chamber placement with conical tips and lower pulse energies (10–20 mJ for PIPS).
- PIPS (photon-induced photoacoustic streaming) positions a conical Er:YAG tip in the pulp chamber using 50 µs pulses at 10–20 mJ; SWEEPS (shock wave enhanced emission photoacoustic streaming) uses double-pulse delivery to generate shock waves even in confined spaces.
- LAI generates intracanal fluid speeds of 0.3–4 m/s, approximately 10 times higher than ultrasonically oscillating files, and can activate irrigant up to 19 mm from the fibre tip.
- Laboratory studies in extracted teeth suggest LAI is superior to conventional syringe irrigation for canal disinfection, with a trend toward higher antimicrobial efficacy compared to ultrasonic activation (UAI).
- Clinical evidence is limited: multiple RCTs show LAI does not increase postoperative pain versus no activation or UAI; only one RCT assessed healing of apical periodontitis (Er;Cr:YSGG vs. conventional), finding no significant difference at 1 year.
- LAI effectively resolves the apical vapour lock effect by progressively breaking down entrapped air bubbles.
- Temperature increases at the apical root surface during LAI are clinically negligible (max ~2.5°C under clinically relevant settings).
- Er:YAG radiation has zero transmission through the dental operating microscope, allowing safe use; wavelength-matched eye protection is still required for all personnel.
- Significant methodological heterogeneity exists across LAI studies, complicating synthesis of results; standardized LAI protocols are lacking.
- A minimal water absorption coefficient of 10 cm⁻¹ is required for effective cavitation-based irrigant activation; this excludes diode lasers (810–1064 nm, alpha < 0.2 cm⁻¹).

## Outcomes

- **Canal disinfection (antimicrobial efficacy) vs. conventional syringe irrigation**: LAI superior to conventional syringe irrigation in the majority of selected laboratory studies in extracted teeth _(statistically significant; clinical: Consistent trend across multiple in vitro studies; clinical confirmation lacking)_
- **Canal disinfection (antimicrobial efficacy) vs. ultrasonic activation (UAI)**: Trend of higher antimicrobial efficacy with LAI vs. UAI; LAI superior in 5/8 studies in one systematic review, no difference in 3/8 _(clinical: Trend favoring LAI but not consistently statistically significant across all studies)_
- **Postoperative pain after LAI vs. no activation or UAI (multiple RCTs)**: No significant difference in postoperative pain levels between LAI and comparators; no untoward events reported _(not statistically significant; clinical: LAI does not increase postoperative pain risk)_
- **Healing of apical periodontitis at 1 year (Er;Cr:YSGG LAI vs. conventional treatment, single RCT)**: No significant difference in apical healing between laser-assisted and conventional groups _(not statistically significant; clinical: Insufficient clinical evidence to confirm LAI benefit on healing outcomes)_
- **Intracanal fluid velocity induced by LAI vs. UAI**: LAI (PIPS) induced fluid speeds of 0.3–0.5 m/s (up to 4 m/s apically), approximately 10 times higher than ultrasonically oscillating file; significant fluid movement up to 19 mm from fibre tip _(clinical: Demonstrates superior irrigant dynamics and remote cleaning capability of LAI)_
- **Temperature change at apical root surface during LAI**: Maximum increase of 2.5°C under clinically relevant settings for both Er:YAG and Er;Cr:YSGG _(clinical: Clinically safe thermal profile)_
- **PIPS vs. SWEEPS efficacy (laboratory studies)**: Mixed results: some studies show no difference, others favor one modality; no consistent superiority of double-pulse over single-pulse LAI _(clinical: Evidence for improved efficacy of SWEEPS over PIPS is currently lacking)_

## Adverse Events

- Potential irrigant extrusion demonstrated in laboratory models with intracanal LAI at high pulse energies
- Increased apical pressure measured in laboratory models during LAI
- LAI activation of NaOCl and EDTA may cause pronounced surface disintegration of root canal dentine (greater than sonic or ultrasonic activation)

## Contraindications

- Diode lasers (810–1064 nm) are not suited for cavitation-based LAI due to insufficient water absorption (alpha < 0.2 cm⁻¹)
- Intracanal fibre placement at high pulse energies raises concerns about irrigant extrusion beyond the apex
