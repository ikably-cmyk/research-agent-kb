## Key Points

- Laser-activated irrigation (LAI) uses pulsed erbium lasers (Er:YAG, Er;Cr:YSGG) to generate cavitation bubbles in root canal irrigants, producing intense fluid dynamics throughout the canal system.
- Cavitation is the primary mechanism of LAI: rapidly growing and imploding vapour bubbles cause high-speed fluid motion, shock waves (in free reservoirs), and secondary cavitation, leading to irrigant penetration into secondary anatomy.
- Key parameters influencing LAI efficacy include: laser wavelength (erbium wavelengths have absorption coefficients of 7000–13,000 cm⁻¹ in water vs. <0.2 cm⁻¹ for diode lasers), pulse energy (directly proportional to bubble size), pulse duration (inversely proportional to bubble size), and fibre tip geometry (conical tips produce spherical bubbles with greater cavitation efficiency than flat tips).
- Diode lasers are not suited for cavitation-based LAI due to extremely low water absorption; only erbium lasers (Er:YAG, Er;Cr:YSGG) and to a lesser extent Nd:YAP (1340 nm) are appropriate.
- Historical trend in LAI: from intracanal fibre placement with flat tips and high pulse energies (60–80 mJ), to coronal/pulp chamber placement with conical tips and lower pulse energies (10–20 mJ for PIPS).
- PIPS (photon-induced photoacoustic streaming) positions a conical tip in the pulp chamber using 50 µs pulses at 10–20 mJ; Biolase/Waterlase protocol uses intracanal placement at 1–5 mm from working length with 25 mJ pulses.
- SWEEPS (shock wave enhanced emission photoacoustic streaming) uses paired Er:YAG pulses to generate shock waves even in confined canal spaces; evidence for superiority over single-pulse LAI is currently lacking.
- Laboratory studies in extracted teeth suggest LAI produces superior canal disinfection compared to conventional syringe irrigation, with a trend toward higher antimicrobial efficacy than ultrasonic activation (UAI).
- Clinical evidence is limited: multiple RCTs show LAI does not increase postoperative pain versus no activation or UAI; only one RCT assessed healing of apical periodontitis (Er;Cr:YSGG vs. conventional, no significant difference at 1 year).
- LAI effectively resolves the apical vapour lock effect by breaking down entrapped air bubbles with each laser pulse.
- Temperature increases at the apical root surface during LAI are minimal (max 2.5°C under clinically relevant settings), supporting safety of use.
- Er:YAG radiation has zero transmission through the dental operating microscope, but wavelength-matched eye protection should always be worn by patient and personnel.
- Significant methodological heterogeneity across LAI studies (varying biofilm models, canal dimensions, irrigants, laser settings, tip designs) complicates synthesis of results and prevents standardized protocol recommendations.
- A minimal water absorption coefficient of 10 cm⁻¹ is required for effective cavitation-based irrigant activation.

## Outcomes

- **Canal disinfection (antimicrobial efficacy) vs. conventional syringe irrigation**: LAI superior to conventional syringe irrigation in the majority of selected laboratory studies _(statistically significant; clinical: Consistent trend across multiple in vitro studies using extracted teeth with dentine biofilm models)_
- **Canal disinfection (antimicrobial efficacy) vs. ultrasonic activation (UAI)**: Trend of higher antimicrobial efficacy with LAI vs. UAI; LAI superior in 5/8 studies in one systematic review, no difference in 3/8 _(clinical: Trend favoring LAI but not consistently significant across all studies)_
- **Postoperative pain after root canal treatment with LAI vs. no activation or UAI**: No significant difference in postoperative pain levels between LAI and comparator groups across multiple RCTs _(not statistically significant; clinical: LAI does not increase postoperative pain risk)_
- **Healing of apical periodontitis (Er;Cr:YSGG LAI vs. conventional treatment)**: No significant difference in apical healing between groups at 1 year (single RCT) _(not statistically significant; clinical: Insufficient clinical evidence to confirm benefit on healing outcomes)_
- **Intracanal fluid velocity induced by LAI vs. ultrasonic activation**: LAI-induced intracanal fluid speeds (0.3–0.5 m/s) approximately 10 times higher than ultrasonically oscillating file; PIPS produced significant fluid movement up to 19 mm from tip vs. <1 mm for ultrasonic _(clinical: Demonstrates superior irrigant dynamics and remote cleaning capability of LAI)_
- **Temperature change at apical root surface during LAI**: Maximum increase of 2.5°C under clinically relevant settings for both Er:YAG and Er;Cr:YSGG _(clinical: Clinically safe thermal profile)_
- **Dentin microhardness after PIPS activation**: No significant effect of PIPS activation on root canal dentin microhardness; effect attributed to irrigant solution, not activation _(not statistically significant; clinical: PIPS activation does not compromise dentin structural integrity)_

## Adverse Events

- Potential irrigant extrusion demonstrated in laboratory models with intracanal LAI placement
- Increased apical pressure measured in laboratory models during LAI
- LAI activation of NaOCl and EDTA may cause pronounced surface disintegration of root canal dentine (greater than sonic or ultrasonic activation)

## Contraindications

- Diode lasers (810–1064 nm) are not suited for cavitation-based LAI due to insufficient water absorption (alpha < 0.2 cm⁻¹)
- Laser tip placement beyond working length raises concerns for irrigant extrusion

## Uncertainties

- Optimal standardized LAI parameters (activation time, tip position, pulse energy, pulse length) remain undefined; no consensus guidelines exist
- The review does not report a single unified protocol but describes multiple protocols from different studies with significant heterogeneity
- Pulse duration for Biolase Waterlase Er;Cr:YSGG recommended protocol (25 mJ) not explicitly stated in the text; frequency also not specified for this protocol
- Clinical evidence on healing of apical periodontitis is limited to one RCT with methodological limitations (different irrigants between groups, no interim dressing in laser group)
- The superiority trend of LAI over UAI is based on laboratory studies with significant methodological heterogeneity; clinical translation remains uncertain
- Conflict of interest (De Moor receiving personal fees from Fotona, manufacturer of Er:YAG lasers/PIPS/SWEEPS systems) may introduce bias in interpretation of results

## Notes

This narrative review applies strict selection criteria to identify the best available laboratory evidence on LAI antimicrobial efficacy (6 studies selected from literature). The review explicitly excludes smear layer removal studies using SEM score-based methods due to methodological deficiencies. The authors note that 'absence of evidence is not evidence of absence' regarding clinical efficacy. A recent systematic review (Meire et al., 2022) found insufficient evidence to recommend any adjunct therapy for apical periodontitis, primarily due to lack of high-quality trials rather than lack of effect.
