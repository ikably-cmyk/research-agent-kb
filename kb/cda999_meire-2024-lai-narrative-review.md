## Key Points

- Laser-activated irrigation (LAI) uses pulsed erbium lasers (Er:YAG, Er;Cr:YSGG) to generate cavitation bubbles in root canal irrigants, producing intense fluid dynamics throughout the canal system.
- Cavitation is the primary mechanism of LAI: rapidly growing and imploding vapour bubbles cause high-speed fluid motion, shock waves (in free reservoirs), and secondary cavitation, leading to irrigant movement and biofilm disruption.
- Key parameters influencing LAI efficacy include: laser wavelength (erbium wavelengths have absorption coefficients of 7000–13,000 cm⁻¹ in water), pulse energy (directly proportional to bubble size), pulse duration (inversely proportional to bubble size), and fibre tip geometry (conical tips produce larger spherical bubbles vs. ellipsoidal bubbles from flat tips).
- Diode lasers (810–1064 nm, alpha <0.2 cm⁻¹) are not suited for cavitation-based LAI due to insufficient water absorption; a minimum absorption coefficient of 10 cm⁻¹ is required.
- Historical trend in LAI: from intracanal fibre placement with flat tips and high pulse energies (60–80 mJ), to coronal/pulp chamber placement with conical tips and lower pulse energies (10–20 mJ for PIPS).
- PIPS (photon-induced photoacoustic streaming) uses a conical tip in the pulp chamber with 50 µs pulses and 10–20 mJ; Biolase/Waterlase Er;Cr:YSGG protocol uses intracanal placement 1–5 mm from working length with 25 mJ pulses.
- SWEEPS (shock wave enhanced emission photoacoustic streaming) uses double Er:YAG pulse pairs to generate shock waves even in confined canal spaces; evidence for superiority over single-pulse LAI is currently lacking.
- Laboratory studies in extracted teeth suggest LAI is superior to conventional syringe irrigation for canal disinfection, and there is a trend of higher antimicrobial efficacy compared to ultrasonic activation (UAI), though significant methodological heterogeneity exists.
- Clinical evidence is limited: multiple RCTs show LAI does not increase postoperative pain versus no activation or UAI; only one RCT assessed healing of apical periodontitis (Er;Cr:YSGG vs. conventional, no significant difference at 1 year).
- LAI effectively resolves the apical vapour lock effect by progressively breaking down entrapped air bubbles.
- Temperature increases at the apical root surface during LAI are clinically safe (max ~2.5°C under clinically relevant settings).
- Er:YAG LAI can be safely used under the dental operating microscope (zero transmission of Er:YAG radiation through the microscope optics); wavelength-matched eye protection is mandatory for all personnel and patients.
- LAI activation of NaOCl and EDTA may cause pronounced surface disintegration of root canal dentine, greater than sonic or ultrasonic activation.
- No standardized LAI protocol exists; significant variability in parameters across studies complicates synthesis of results.

## Outcomes

- **Canal disinfection (antimicrobial efficacy) vs. conventional syringe irrigation**: LAI superior to conventional irrigation in the majority of selected laboratory studies (extracted teeth) _(statistically significant; clinical: Consistent trend across multiple in vitro studies, but clinical confirmation lacking)_
- **Canal disinfection (antimicrobial efficacy) vs. ultrasonic activation (UAI)**: Trend of higher antimicrobial efficacy with LAI vs. UAI; LAI superior in 5/8 studies in one systematic review, no difference in 3/8 _(clinical: Trend favoring LAI but not consistently significant across all studies)_
- **Postoperative pain after LAI vs. no activation or UAI**: No higher postoperative pain with LAI in multiple RCTs; some RCTs found lower pain scores with LAI systems vs. other activation methods _(statistically significant; clinical: LAI does not increase postoperative pain risk)_
- **Healing of apical periodontitis (Er;Cr:YSGG LAI vs. conventional treatment)**: No significant difference in apical healing between groups at 1 year (single RCT) _(not statistically significant; clinical: Insufficient clinical evidence to confirm benefit on healing outcomes)_
- **Intracanal fluid velocity induced by LAI vs. UAI**: LAI (PIPS) induced fluid speeds 0.3–0.5 m/s, approximately 10 times higher than ultrasonically oscillating file; apical region speeds 1–4 m/s; significant fluid movement up to 19 mm from fibre tip _(clinical: Demonstrates superior irrigant dynamics and remote cleaning capability of LAI)_
- **Temperature change at apical root surface during LAI**: Maximum increase of 2.5°C under clinically relevant settings for both Er:YAG and Er;Cr:YSGG _(clinical: Clinically safe thermal profile)_
- **Dentin microhardness after PIPS activation**: No significant difference in dentinal microhardness between PIPS-activated and non-activated irrigation solutions _(not statistically significant; clinical: PIPS activation does not adversely affect dentin hardness)_
- **Root canal dentine surface disintegration with LAI vs. sonic/ultrasonic activation**: LAI activation of NaOCl and EDTA led to pronounced surface disintegration, higher than sonic (Eddy) or ultrasonic activation _(statistically significant; clinical: Potential concern for dentin integrity with LAI; requires further investigation)_
